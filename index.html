What is AI?

The "Zip File of the Internet" Mental Model

Think of Large Language Models (LLMs) like ChatGPT as a "zip file" of the internet. During training, the AI reads essentially all of the internet and compresses that knowledge into its neural network. When you ask a question, it's recalling information from this compressed knowledge base.

This compression happens through a process called pre-training, where the model learns patterns from massive amounts of text. Then, through post-training, human labelers fine-tune it to respond helpfully as an assistant.

Understanding Tokens: How AI Reads Text

AI doesn't read words the way we do. It breaks text into tokens - small chunks that can be parts of words, whole words, or punctuation. A short sentence might be 15 tokens, while a paragraph could be 50-100 tokens.

Why does this matter?

  • Longer conversations use more tokens, which can slow things down
  • There's a limit to how many tokens AI can process at once (the "context window")
  • Understanding tokens helps you write more efficient prompts

Try it yourself: Tik Tokenizer lets you see exactly how your text gets broken into tokens.

The Context Window: AI's Working Memory

When you chat with AI, you're building a one-dimensional token sequence together. Your messages + AI's responses = the conversation history. This is called the context window - it's the AI's working memory for your conversation.

  • AI remembers everything in your current chat
  • When you start a new chat, you wipe that memory and start fresh
  • Starting fresh is often good - it keeps the AI focused on your current task

Tip: Start a new chat when switching to a completely different topic. This prevents the AI from being distracted by old content and can improve response quality.

Probabilistic Recall: Why AI Can Be Wrong

AI doesn't "remember" facts like a database looks up records. It probabilistically reconstructs information based on patterns. This means:

  • It can occasionally get facts wrong (called "hallucinations")
  • It may sound confident even when incorrect
  • Common information is more reliable than obscure details
  • Always verify important information against primary sources

Two Critical Limitations

By default, when you talk to an AI, you're talking to a self-contained entity. There's no calculator, no internet connection, no access to your files - just a neural network predicting helpful responses based on its compressed knowledge. This creates two important limitations:

1. Knowledge Cutoff Dates

AI models are trained at a specific point in time. They don't automatically know about:

  • Recent news or events
  • New company policies or job postings
  • Updated salary information
  • Current market conditions

2. Niche Information Gaps

AI excels at common knowledge but struggles with specialized, local information:

  • Specific company cultures and hiring practices
  • Local job market conditions
  • Regional employer preferences
  • Your specific school's policies

The good news? These limitations have solutions. Modern AI tools come equipped with additional capabilities that extend what they can do far beyond their base training...

Tools AI Can Use

Modern AI isn't limited to just its training knowledge. It can use external tools to search the web, run calculations, analyze files, and more. Understanding these tools helps you get much better results.

Internet Search

Remember the knowledge cutoff problem? Web search solves this by letting AI access current information from the internet in real-time.

How It Works

  1. You ask a question that needs current information
  2. The AI emits a special "search the internet" signal
  3. It searches the web and loads relevant pages into its context
  4. It synthesizes an answer from those fresh sources

When to use search:

  • "When are the registration deadlines for [college]?"
  • "What's the current average salary for medical assistants in Arkansas?"
  • "What companies are hiring in [city] right now?"
  • Any question where the answer might have changed recently

Tools with search:

Tool Search Capability
Perplexity Built specifically for search - always searches and cites sources
ChatGPT Has web search (automatic or manual toggle)
Gemini Integrated with Google search
Claude Web search available (added in 2025)

Tip: When you know you need current information, explicitly ask for a search or use Perplexity, rather than hoping the model auto-detects the need.

Deep Research Mode

For complex questions, some AI tools offer Deep Research - a combination of internet search and extended thinking that runs for 5-15+ minutes. The AI:

  • Issues many internet searches across different angles
  • Reads through papers, articles, and documents
  • Thinks deeply about the information
  • Produces a comprehensive report with citations

Great for:

  • Industry analysis for career exploration
  • Deep company research (culture, growth, opportunities)
  • Comparing programs, certifications, or schools
  • Understanding emerging career fields

Important caveat: Even Deep Research can miss things or make mistakes. Treat the output as a well-researched first draft, not ground truth. Always follow up on citations for critical decisions.

Code Execution (Python Interpreter)

LLMs can recognize problems they can't solve "in their head" and write programs to calculate the answer instead.

Simple math (30 x 9): The model calculates in its head - works fine.

Complex calculations (large numbers, statistics): The model writes Python code, runs it, and returns the accurate result.

What this enables:

  • Data analysis - Upload a spreadsheet, ask for insights
  • Creating charts and graphs - Visualize trends
  • Accurate calculations - No estimation errors
  • Processing files - Extract and transform data

Warning: Not all AI tools have code execution. If a model doesn't have calculator access, it will still try to answer math questions and may give plausible-looking but incorrect results. Always verify numerical calculations for important work.

File Uploads

You can upload documents directly to AI tools and ask questions about them. The content gets converted to tokens and loaded into the context window.

Supported file types:

  • PDFs - Reports, articles, handouts, research papers
  • Word documents - Drafts, policies, lesson plans
  • Spreadsheets - Data for analysis
  • Images - Screenshots, diagrams, photos of text
  • Code files - For technical users

Practical uses for advisors:

  • Upload a confusing policy document: "Summarize the key points"
  • Share a student's resume draft: "What improvements would you suggest?"
  • Upload a job posting: "What skills should a student highlight for this role?"
  • Share a research paper: "Explain the main findings in simple terms"

Two-step best practice:

  1. First, ask the model to transcribe/extract the key information
  2. Verify the extraction is correct
  3. Then ask your analytical questions

This ensures the model actually "saw" the content correctly before you trust its analysis.

Modalities: Different Ways to Interact with AI

AI isn't limited to text anymore. Modern LLMs can work with multiple modalities - different types of input and output including text, voice, and images.

Text (The Default)

Text is the foundation of AI interaction. You type a message, the AI responds with text. This is what most people think of when they imagine using ChatGPT or similar tools.

Best for: Most tasks - writing, planning, brainstorming, explaining, analyzing.

Voice: Speech Input and Output

Speech-to-Text Input

Instead of typing, you can speak your questions and the AI transcribes them:

  • On mobile: Tap the microphone icon, speak your query
  • On desktop: Some tools have built-in voice; others work with system apps like SuperWhisper or Whisper Flow

Why it matters: Voice input is much faster for many people. Some power users report 50-80% of their queries are voice rather than typed.

Text-to-Speech Output

Most AI apps have a "Read Aloud" feature to speak responses back to you. Useful for accessibility or when you want to listen while doing something else.

Advanced Voice Mode (Real Conversations)

Tools like ChatGPT have Advanced Voice Mode that handles audio natively - not just speech-to-text-to-speech, but actual audio processing:

  • Real-time conversation with very low latency
  • Natural back-and-forth like talking to a person
  • Can express emotion and engagement

Student Use Case: Mock Interview Practice

This is perfect for students who need interview practice. They can:

  • Practice phone-style interviews on their own time
  • Get unlimited repetitions without scheduling hassles
  • Practice at home where they have phone access
  • Receive feedback after the conversation

Image Input: Seeing and Analyzing

AI can "see" images you upload - photos, screenshots, diagrams, documents. The image gets converted into tokens (through a process of breaking it into patches) and the AI can analyze what it sees.

Practical examples:

  • Nutrition labels or documents: Upload a photo, get a breakdown of contents
  • Screenshots: Share what's on your screen, ask questions about it
  • Math from papers: Screenshot an equation, get it explained or solved
  • Handwritten notes: Upload a photo, get it transcribed
  • Confusing memes: Upload an image, get the joke explained

For advisors: Take a photo of a flyer, event schedule, or document and ask "What are the key details here?" or "Create a summary I can share with students."

Image Output: Generation

AI can also create images based on your descriptions. ChatGPT has native image generation built in.

Use cases:

  • Creating simple graphics or icons
  • Thumbnails for presentations
  • Visual aids for lessons
  • Creative content

For most visual work, Canva is still the better choice - it's designed for creating polished materials. But AI image generation is useful for quick visuals or when you need something specific that doesn't exist in templates.

Video Input: Point and Talk

On mobile, ChatGPT's Advanced Voice Mode can use your camera in real-time:

  • Point your camera at something
  • Ask questions about what you're showing
  • Get responses while the camera is active

This is newer and still evolving, but it opens interesting possibilities for real-time assistance.

Real Use Cases from Rooted Advisors

These examples come directly from advisors who are already using AI to make their work easier.

1. Event Planning Timelines

The Challenge: An advisor needed to plan a career fair with 40 businesses. She admits she "hates event planning" and isn't detail-oriented.

The Solution: She asked ChatGPT to create a complete event timeline. The AI generated:

  • Setup windows and vendor arrival times
  • Junior and senior class walkthrough schedules
  • Lunch periods and breakdown logistics
  • Building exit procedures

The Result: "This now gave me the simplest path forward. I just needed to tweak it to our bell schedule."

Why it works: AI handles the tedious details so you can focus on what matters most.

2. Outreach Emails and Communication

The Challenge: Reaching out to businesses, explaining Rooted, and inviting speakers takes significant mental energy and time.

The Solution: Give AI a few sentences about what you want to say, and ask it to write a professional email.

  • Turn 2-3 bullet points into a complete, professional message
  • Rephrase, shorten, or formalize existing drafts
  • Create reusable templates for recurring outreach

The Result: "This takes the brain suck power out of it. Give it three sentences and tell it to beef it up."

Why it works: Removes the friction of "what do I say?" so you actually send those important emails.

3. Website Scavenger Hunts and Activities

The Challenge: Creating engaging activities that connect to local resources takes hours of planning.

The Solution: An advisor found a local community website and asked AI to create a scavenger hunt from it.

  • 15 search-and-find questions generated instantly
  • Complete answer key included
  • Options for multiple choice or open-ended formats

The Result: "A creative project that would literally take hours to game plan... boom, it's done."

Why it works: Your local knowledge + AI's speed = activities that actually connect to your community.

4. Mock Interview Practice (Voice Mode)

The Challenge: Students need interview practice, but advisors lack time for one-on-one sessions and business partners can't always volunteer.

The Solution: AI tools with voice mode can simulate phone interviews. Students can practice anytime.

  • ChatGPT's Advanced Voice Mode supports real conversations
  • Students practice without scheduling constraints
  • Unlimited practice opportunities

The Result: "This cuts out the miscommunication and timing it takes to find someone willing to volunteer an hour."

Why it works: Students get the practice they need, when they need it.

Ready-to-Use Prompts

Copy these prompts and customize them for your needs. Replace the [bracketed text] with your specific information.

Event Planning Timeline

I'm planning a [career fair/event type] at [school name]. We have [number] businesses attending. The event will be in the [gym/location]. Our school schedule: - School starts at [time] - Lunch periods are [times] - School ends at [time] Please create a detailed timeline including: - Setup time for staff - Vendor/business arrival and setup - Student walkthrough times by grade - Breakdown and cleanup Make it minute-by-minute where it matters.

Outreach Email

Help me write a professional email to invite a local business to [speak at our school/participate in our career fair/mentor students]. Key points to include: - I work with Heartland Forward's Rooted program - We connect high school students with career opportunities - [Specific ask: date, time, what you need from them] Keep it friendly but professional. About 150-200 words.

Website Scavenger Hunt

Create a scavenger hunt activity using this website: [paste URL] Generate 15 questions that students can answer by exploring the website. Include: - A mix of easy and challenging questions - Questions about careers, requirements, and opportunities - An answer key at the end Format it as a worksheet I can print for students.

Mock Interview Practice

I want you to act as an interviewer for a [job type] position at a local [industry] company. Conduct a realistic phone interview with me. Ask one question at a time, wait for my response, then give brief feedback and ask the next question. Start with an introduction and ask 5-7 common interview questions. At the end, give me overall feedback on my answers. Begin when I say "I'm ready."

Quick Lesson Ideas

I have [20/30/45] minutes with [junior/senior] students. The topic is [career exploration/resume writing/interview skills/workplace communication]. Give me 3 different activity ideas that: - Require minimal prep - Keep students engaged - Can be done with [Chromebooks/paper and pencil/no technology] Include brief instructions I can follow.

Tips for Getting Better Results

Be Specific

The more context you give, the better the output. Instead of "write an email," try "write a professional email to invite a local bank manager to speak at our career day on March 15th."

Iterate and Refine

Your first result doesn't have to be perfect. Ask AI to:

  • "Make this shorter"
  • "Make it more formal"
  • "Add more detail about [topic]"
  • "Rewrite this for a [different audience]"

Start Fresh for New Topics

When you switch to a completely different task, start a new chat. This helps the AI stay focused on your current need.

The "LLM Council" Approach

For important decisions, consider asking multiple AI tools the same question and comparing responses. When they agree, you can be more confident. When they disagree, you know to dig deeper.

Always Review the Output

AI is a powerful assistant, but you're the expert on your students and community. Review everything before using it, and make adjustments as needed.
